## Cluster Details

### Cluster 1: Tracking System Deception

**Theme:** The real-time tracking app and physical tableau at the stop consistently display false arrival times — counting down normally before resetting, freezing, or silently disappearing. Students have learned to distrust these tools but have nothing to replace them with.

**Sample insights:**

1. "I arrived at the stop, when I see that number three is coming in five minutes. Five minutes pass and countdown starts again and it says 59 minutes." (Dachi)
2. "It is usually 10 or 15 minutes at minimum late, later than the tableau says. People base their arrival time on the tableau." (David)
3. "It said five minutes, then four minutes, then stuck on one minute for ten minutes — and eventually it said cancelled." (Dimitri)
4. "I stopped using it after it told me the bus was two minutes away for thirty minutes straight. I just stood there like an idiot. Now I just go early and hope." (Mariam)
5. "At first, it said 16 minutes. Then another 16 minutes appeared. I waited for an hour. It was not 16 minutes." (MariamP)
6. "The tableau is a suggestion, not a fact. It counted down from 5 minutes to 1, stayed at 1 for ten minutes, then disappeared from the screen entirely." (Davit)

**Initial observation:** This is the largest cluster and arguably the most important finding in the dataset. The failure mode is not passive — the tools hold students at the stop on false confidence until it is too late to seek an alternative. A countdown that resets to 59 minutes, or a bus that vanishes from the screen, is actively worse than no information because it delays the correct decision. Several participants have already abandoned the app entirely. Any solution that does not establish honest accuracy from day one will be categorised as another failed tool before it has a chance.

### Cluster 2: Extreme Early-Arrival Routines

**Theme:** Every participant has independently arrived at the same coping strategy — leave home far earlier than necessary and accept the wasted time as the cost of certainty. Buffers of 1.5 to 3 hours are described uniformly across all 12 interviews.

**Sample insights:**

1. "I think I have to get early like — if I have a seminar at 9am, I have to leave at 7am. It is frustrating." (Dachi)
2. "I usually miss about at minimum an hour just getting from Kutaisi to KIU. That would be about five hours a week." (David)
3. "I wake up at least three hours before my lecture. If I have a 9am, I get up at 6am. It is actually very terrible because it is troubling my sleep schedule." (Dimitri)
4. "I basically treat the bus schedule as a suggestion. I know it says 8:15 but I leave by 7 at the latest because I just cannot trust it anymore. It has been like this since my first year." (Mariam)
5. "I've started leaving Terjola way too early. I arrive in Kutaisi at 8:30 even if my class is at 11:00. I spend so much time sitting in the KIU lobby because I'm scared to trust the schedule." (NikolozK)
6. "If I have a 9:00am class, I get up at 7:00 to go to the bus station and be there by 8:00. So I need at least one or two hours to get here." (MariamP)

**Initial observation:** The universality of this pattern across all 12 participants — regardless of year, origin, or schedule — makes it the strongest behavioural signal in the study. David's self-calculated figure of five wasted hours per week, offered unprompted, shows that students are fully aware of the cost but see no alternative. Dimitri's sleep disruption shows the problem has crossed from lifestyle inconvenience into physiological harm. Early arrival is not a preference; it is a tax that every Bus #3 commuter pays daily.

### Cluster 3: Direct Academic Consequences

**Theme:** Bus unreliability produces concrete, measurable academic harm — missed graded assessments, GPA drops, and in the most serious cases, a generalised disengagement from academic sessions that follows a particularly bad commute.

**Sample insights:**

1. "It affected my GPA. I was late on quizzes, seminars, so scores went down and GPA went down." (Dachi)
2. "I got there an hour and a half early and the bus didn't arrive at all. I had to wait an hour for the second one." (David)
3. "Every morning is a gamble. I have lost maybe four or five quizzes this semester just from being late." (Giorgi)
4. "I ran to the stop and saw the bus pulling away. I waved like crazy but the driver didn't stop. I missed half of my seminar. I walked in, sat in the back, and didn't even open my laptop. What's the point?" (TsiraB)
5. "Sometimes the bus is late and I have to explain myself why I'm late on the quiz." (SabaM)
6. "If the bus is more than 20 minutes late, I have to choose between spending my lunch money on a taxi or being marked absent. It's not really a choice; it's a penalty for living off-campus." (Davit)

**Initial observation:** This cluster contains the highest-stakes consequences in the dataset. GPA impacts are long-term and often irreversible within a semester. Missed graded assessments frequently require bureaucratic negotiation to remedy (SabaM had to sit a quiz with a different group, extending his campus day by an hour). Most significantly, Tsira's "What's the point?" moment signals that the problem is generating academic disengagement — a downstream harm that persists even after the student arrives. A student who stops opening their laptop in seminars has crossed from a transport problem into a student welfare problem.

### Cluster 4: Financial Trade-Offs and the Taxi Trap

**Theme:** When Bus #3 fails, the only reliable fallback is a Maxim taxi costing 6–7 GEL — directly competing with food and other daily essentials. There is no middle-cost option. Late-cancellation notices compound this by forcing students into the most expensive taxi tier at the worst possible moment.

**Sample insights:**

1. "I had to call a taxi — not just a regular taxi but an express taxi, which costed me extra funds. As a university student I don't have that kind of funds to expand upon on a daily basis." (Dimitri)
2. "I had four lari on me. The taxi was six, so I had to call my mum and ask her to send money, which was really embarrassing. It is not like we are poor, it is just I should not have to do this for a bus." (Mariam)
3. "If I spend 6 or 7 lari on a taxi, that's my lunch money gone. I'll be eating semichka for dinner." (TsiraB)
4. "I waited because I couldn't do anything else. I didn't have money for a taxi, so I just waited." (MariamP)
5. "If the bus is more than 20 minutes late, I have to choose between spending my lunch money on a taxi or being marked absent." (Davit)

**Initial observation:** The taxi is not a neutral alternative — it is an emergency measure that trades one harm (being late) for another (financial stress or food insecurity). The shame dimension that Mariam introduces is worth noting separately: calling a parent for taxi money is not just a financial event, it is a relational and dignity cost that quantitative analysis misses. Dimitri's express taxi surcharge is a particularly acute case — the late cancellation notice on the tableau forced him into the most expensive option at the exact moment he had the fewest choices. Earlier, more honest information would have allowed a cheaper alternative.

### Cluster 5: Physical Environment as a Hidden Cost

**Theme:** The bus itself is overcrowded and poorly ventilated; the stops offer no shade, shelter, or connectivity. Students arrive at campus depleted, not ready. The commute acts as a drain on the mental energy needed for the academic day.

**Sample insights:**

1. "I physically could not breathe. There were so many people packed in there. I had to get down because I couldn't breathe and otherwise I was going to faint." (MariamK)
2. "It is a greenhouse on wheels. By the time we reach the campus gates, the windows are fogged up from so many people breathing in a small space. It's hard to focus on a coding lab after that." (Davit)
3. "No, it is very jam-packed. The bus is late often. There's not enough air or conditioning." (MariamK)
4. "I just start walking and hope a taxi comes by. Sometimes I just walk the whole way and arrive sweating and then I have to sit in class like that." (Giorgi)
5. "I'm standing there, it's freezing or raining, and I scroll TikTok. I could be in my bed in Samtredia, but instead I'm standing on a random sidewalk waiting for a bus that might not even exist." (TsiraB)
6. "Since I work online, my time is literally money. I'm standing at the stop for 30 minutes and I can't open my laptop on the street." (NikolozK)

**Initial observation:** This cluster is distinct from the timing problem and should not be collapsed into it. MariamK's near-fainting episode represents a genuine health and safety failure, not merely inconvenience. Giorgi's dignity cost — arriving sweating, sitting through class in that state — is a real but rarely documented harm. NikolozK's situation is perhaps the starkest: he loses billable work hours standing at a stop with no shade or connectivity. These physical conditions mean the bus stop is not a neutral waiting space; it is an active source of harm for each additional minute students spend there. Solutions that reduce unnecessary waiting time address this cluster directly.

### Cluster 6: Intercity Commuter Vulnerability

**Theme:** Students arriving from Terjola or Samtredia depend on a two-leg journey in which Bus #3 is the second, unforgiving stage. Any upstream delay — a late marshrutka, traffic — forfeits the connection with no recovery option. These students bear the highest cost of each system failure.

**Sample insights:**

1. "I wake up at like 7:00 AM in Samtredia. I catch a marshrutka to get into Kutaisi. By the time I get to the #3 bus stop, it's already 8:00 AM. I just stand there leaning against a wall, hoping the bus shows up so I don't miss my first lecture." (TsiraB)
2. "I wake up early because I have a full-time online job. Then I catch a marshrutka from Terjola to the Kutaisi entrance. But from there, I have to get Bus #3 to reach the campus." (NikolozK)
3. "My marshrutka from Samtredia was late because of traffic. I ran to the #3 stop and saw the bus pulling away." (TsiraB)
4. "I arrive in Kutaisi at 8:30 even if my class is at 11:00. I sit in the KIU lobby for hours because I'm scared to trust the schedule." (NikolozK)

**Initial observation:** This is the smallest cluster by sticky count but represents the most extreme failure mode in the dataset. For NikolozK and TsiraB, a missed Bus #3 is not a 30-minute inconvenience — it is the loss of an entire morning that began before 7am. The transfer point at the Kutaisi city entrance is a documented gap in the system: university shuttles do not serve it, Bus #3 does not synchronise with marshrutka schedules, and there is no infrastructure to support the wait. This sub-group is underrepresented in our sample and likely in transit planning more broadly. A solution that works reliably for intercity commuters — who need pre-departure information before a 60-minute marshrutka ride — is necessarily better than one designed only for city-based students.

### Cluster 7: Students Have Already Diagnosed the Solution

**Theme:** Participants have independently and accurately separated the problem they can solve (information honesty) from the one they cannot (bus operator performance). One participant named the precise product requirement unprompted and stated explicit willingness to pay.

**Sample insights:**

1. "Honestly, just tell me the truth. Not 'three minutes' when you mean twenty. Not 'on time' when you mean cancelled. I do not need it to be perfect. I just need it to be honest, and I will plan around that." (Nino)
2. "I would pay good money for a bus app that just told the truth." (Nino — unprompted)
3. "By the third day I just sat at the stop and kind of laughed. I had done everything right. I had left on time, I had checked the app, I had built in extra time. And still." (Nino)
4. "Would the student be willing to pay for a private shuttle if it guaranteed a 9:45 AM arrival?" (Interviewer open question — NikolozK log)

**Initial observation:** This is the smallest cluster by sticky count but the highest signal-density finding in the study. Nino's formulation — "I don't need it to be perfect, I just need it to be honest" — is the clearest articulation of user need across all 12 interviews, and it reframes the problem precisely. The issue is not that the bus is late; it is that students cannot plan around a system that lies to them. If the information were honest, students say they could cope. This distinction is critical: it moves the solution space from "fix the bus" (impossible for a student project) to "fix the information" (tractable). Her unprompted willingness to pay is the only payment-intent signal in the full dataset and should be probed directly in the next interview round.

## Outliers and Surprises

1. "I mentioned it to my academic advisor once and she kind of smiled and said yes, it is a known issue. That was the whole conversation." (Mariam) — Institutional awareness without institutional action. The university has absorbed the problem as a fixture rather than treating it as something to address. This matters because it signals there is no top-down fix coming, which strengthens the case for a student-led solution.

2. "I got to the bus station, but the board that usually says when buses arrive was saying that they weren't working that day, so I had to ask some locals where I could catch another bus." (SabaM) — During roadworks, the digital signage displayed only "not working" with no rerouting information. The board failed at the exact moment it was most needed. Students fell back on tribal knowledge from passers-by.

3. "I make sure to not schedule any tutoring lessons at least two to three hours after I'm supposed to get home. I feel like I'm constantly in a waiting mode." (MariamK) — The commute's unpredictability is not contained to the morning journey. It cascades forward into the evening, preventing students from taking on paid work. This makes the hidden economic cost of unreliability larger than it first appears.

4. "A friend moved closer to campus specifically because of the bus. I described it as 'drastic but I understand her.'" (Mariam) — Bus #3 unreliability is severe enough to influence housing decisions. This is a significant signal of unmet need and the clearest evidence that the problem has real-world consequences beyond daily inconvenience.
