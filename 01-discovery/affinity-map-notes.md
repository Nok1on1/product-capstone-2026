## Clusters

### Cluster 1: The tracking app and tableau actively mislead students, destroying their ability to plan

Sticky count: 16

Representative quotes:

- "I arrived at a stop, usually I arrive at stop, when I see that number three is coming in five minutes. Five minutes pass and countdown starts again and it says 59 minutes." (Interview 1, Dachi)
- "It is usually 10 or 15 minutes at minimum late, later than the tableau says. So that is really frustrating because sometimes people usually base their arrival time on the tableau." (Interview 2, David)
- "It said five minutes, then it said four minutes and the countdown went quite well, but it was stuck on one minute for at least like ten minutes and eventually it said cancelled." (Interview 3, Dimitri)
- "I stopped using it after it told me the bus was two minutes away for thirty minutes straight. I just stood there like an idiot." (Interview 4, Mariam)
- "At first, it said like 16 minutes... then those 16 minutes went by really slowly, and then another 16 minutes appeared. I waited for an hour. It was not 16 minutes." (Interview 8, MariamP)
- "The tableau is a suggestion, not a fact. Last Tuesday, it counted down from 5 minutes to 1 minute, stayed at 1 minute for ten minutes, and then just disappeared from the screen entirely." (Interview 9, Davit)

What this cluster suggests: The information problem is worse than a simple absence of data — the tools present false confidence right up to the moment of failure. Students trust the countdown enough to stand and wait; by the time the cancellation appears or the bus simply vanishes from the board, it is too late to seek an alternative. The failure mode is active deception, not passive silence. Any solution must provide honest, accurate data or risk compounding this exact pattern of betrayed trust.

### Cluster 2: Students have built extreme early-arrival routines as their primary coping strategy — at significant cost to sleep, time, and wellbeing

Sticky count: 14

Representative quotes:

- "I think I have to get early like if I have a seminar on 9 a.m. I have to get early at 7 a.m. so this is frustrating." (Interview 1, Dachi)
- "I usually miss about at minimum an hour on going from Kutaisi to KIU, so that would be about five hours a week just to get to university." (Interview 2, David)
- "I wake up at least three hours before my lecture. So if I have it on 9am, I get up at 6am. It is actually very terrible because it is kind of troubling on my sleep schedule." (Interview 3, Dimitri)
- "I basically treat the bus schedule as a suggestion. I know it says 8:15 but I leave by 7 at the latest because I just cannot trust it anymore." (Interview 4, Mariam)
- "I've started leaving Terjola way too early. Like, I arrive in Kutaisi at 8:30 even if my class is at 11:00. I spend so much time sitting in the KIU lobby because I'm scared to trust the schedule." (Interview 10, NikolozK)
- "If I have a 9:00 a.m. class, I get up at 7:00 to go to the bus station and be there by 8:00. So I need at least one or two hours to get here." (Interview 8, MariamP)

What this cluster suggests: Early arrival has become the universal and fully normalised coping strategy. Students are not arriving two to three hours early by preference — they are doing so because the system has made it the only rational response to irreducible uncertainty. David's self-calculated figure of five wasted hours per week, volunteered unprompted, signals that students are aware of the cost but feel there is no alternative. Sleep disruption (Dimitri) shows the problem has crossed from inconvenience into physiological harm.

### Cluster 3: Bus unreliability causes direct academic consequences — missed quizzes, GPA drops, and late-session disengagement

Sticky count: 11

Representative quotes:

- "It affected my GPA. I mean, I was late on quizzes, seminars, so scores went down and GPA went down." (Interview 1, Dachi)
- "I got there an hour and a half early and the bus didn't arrive at all. And I had to wait one hour for the second bus to arrive — so that was also a big problem." (Interview 2, David)
- "Back home the buses were not perfect either but at least you kind of knew. Here I feel like every morning is a gamble. I have lost maybe four or five quizzes this semester just from being late." (Interview 5, Giorgi)
- "I ran to the #3 stop, and I saw the bus pulling away. I waved like crazy, but the driver didn't give a f*. He just kept going. I had to wait 30 minutes for the next one. I missed half of my seminar. I just walked in, sat in the back, and didn't even open my laptop." (Interview 11, TsiraB)
- "Well, sometimes the bus is late and I have to explain myself why I'm late on the quiz at the university." (Interview 12, SabaM)
- "If the bus is more than 20 minutes late, I have to choose between spending my lunch money on a taxi or being marked absent. It's not really a choice; it's a penalty for living off-campus." (Interview 9, Davit)

What this cluster suggests: This is the highest-stakes consequence category across all twelve interviews. The problem has moved beyond inconvenience into measurable academic harm. GPA impacts are long-term; missed graded assessments are often irrecoverable without bureaucratic negotiation. Critically, Tsira's "What's the point?" reaction after missing a seminar suggests that unreliability is causing not just lateness but academic disengagement — a downstream consequence that is harder to reverse than a single missed quiz.

### Cluster 4: When the bus fails, students face a forced financial trade-off between a taxi and basic needs

Sticky count: 9

Representative quotes:

- "I had to call taxi — not just regular taxi but also express taxi which costed me extra funds and as a university student I don't have that kind of funds to expand upon on daily basis." (Interview 3, Dimitri)
- "I had four lari on me. Taxi was six, so I had to call my mum and ask her to send money, which was really embarrassing." (Interview 4, Mariam)
- "I just pray. Seriously... If I spend 6 or 7 lari on a taxi, that's my lunch money gone. I'll be eating 'semichka' for dinner." (Interview 11, TsiraB)
- "I waited because I couldn't do anything else. I didn't have money for a taxi, so I just waited." (Interview 8, MariamP)
- "If the bus is more than 20 minutes late, I have to choose between spending my lunch money on a taxi or being marked absent." (Interview 9, Davit)

What this cluster suggests: The taxi is not a neutral alternative — it is an emergency option that directly competes with food and other basic needs. The pattern across multiple interviews is consistent: for students without disposable income, bus failure forces a zero-sum choice. Dimitri's experience with express taxi surcharges shows that late-cancellation notifications compound the financial harm, pushing students into the most expensive option at the worst possible moment. There is no middle-cost option between the free bus and an expensive taxi.

### Cluster 5: The physical environment of the bus and bus stop adds a health and dignity cost on top of the timing problem

Sticky count: 7

Representative quotes:

- "I physically could not breathe. There were so many people packed in there... I had to get down because I couldn't breathe and otherwise I was going to faint." (Interview 7, MariamK)
- "It is a greenhouse on wheels. By the time we reach the campus gates, the windows are fogged up from so many people breathing in a small space. It's hard to focus on a coding lab after that." (Interview 9, Davit)
- "No, it is very jam-packed. The bus is late often... There's not enough air or conditioning going on in there." (Interview 7, MariamK)
- "Honestly I just start walking and hope a taxi comes by... sometimes I just walk the whole way and arrive sweating and then I have to sit in class like that." (Interview 5, Giorgi)
- "I'm standing there, it's freezing or raining, and I scroll TikTok. I could be in my bed in Samtredia, but instead, I'm standing on a random sidewalk in Kutaisi waiting for a bus that might not even exist." (Interview 11, TsiraB)
- "Since I work online, my time is literally money. I'm standing at the stop for 30 minutes waiting for the #3, I can't open my laptop on the street." (Interview 10, NikolozK)

What this cluster suggests: The commute degrades students before they arrive. Overcrowded buses create health risks (MariamK's near-fainting episode). Walking as a fallback creates a dignity cost — arriving dishevelled and sweating. Waiting in cold, rain, or direct sunlight compounds the mental toll of delay. Students are not arriving at campus ready to learn; they are arriving in recovery mode. This is a distinct harm category from timing and should be considered separately in any solution framing.

### Cluster 6: Intercity commuters face compounded vulnerability — the transit system has no tolerance for upstream delays

Sticky count: 4

Representative quotes:

- "I wake up at like 7:00 AM in Samtredia. I have to catch a marshrutka to get into Kutaisi. By the time I get to the #3 bus stop, it's already 8:00 AM. I just stand there leaning against a wall, hoping the bus shows up so I don't miss my first lecture." (Interview 11, TsiraB)
- "I wake up early because I have a full-time online job. Then I catch a marshrutka from Terjola to the Kutaisi entrance. But from there, I have to get Bus #3 to reach the campus." (Interview 10, NikolozK)
- "My marshrutka from Samtredia was late because of traffic. I ran to the #3 stop, and I saw the bus pulling away." (Interview 11, TsiraB)
- "The transfer point at the entrance of Kutaisi is a major friction point where students feel most vulnerable." (Interview 10, NikolozK — interviewer observation)

What this cluster suggests: For students commuting from Terjola or Samtredia, Bus #3 is the second leg of a multi-stage journey. Any delay in the marshrutka forfeits the Bus #3 connection, and there is no buffer built into the system. These students are the most exposed: they have already invested significant travel time before they ever reach the Bus #3 stop, and a missed connection doubles or triples their morning. This sub-group has been underrepresented in solution thinking to date.

### Cluster 7 (unexpected): Students already know what they need from a solution — honest information, not perfection

Sticky count: 4 (but high signal density)

Representative quotes:

- "Honestly, just tell me the truth. Not 'three minutes' when you mean twenty. Not 'on time' when you mean cancelled. I do not need it to be perfect. I just need it to be honest, and I will plan around that." (Interview 6, Nino)
- "I would pay good money for a bus app that just told the truth." (Interview 6, Nino — unprompted)
- "By the third day I just sat at the stop and kind of laughed. I had done everything right. I had left on time, I had checked the app, I had built in extra time. And still." (Interview 6, Nino)
- "Would the student be willing to pay for a private shuttle if it guaranteed a 9:45 AM arrival?" (Interview 10, NikolozK — open question in log)

What this cluster suggests: Nino's articulation — "I don't need it to be perfect, I just need it to be honest" — is the clearest direct statement of user need across all twelve interviews. She has accurately diagnosed the problem and proposed the solution herself: reliable information at the point of decision-making, so she can plan around the real situation rather than a false one. Crucially, she expressed unprompted willingness to pay for this. This is the first and only explicit payment-intent signal across the full dataset and should be probed directly in the next interview round.

## Pattern Analysis

**Largest cluster:** Cluster 1 (16 stickies — tracking system deception). Concentration confirms that the core problem is not just bus unreliability; it is information unreliability layered on top of it. The tableau and app don't merely fail — they make things worse by holding students at the stop while false data plays out. Any solution must address this failure mode directly or it will be dismissed as another misleading tool.

**Most emotionally charged cluster:** Cluster 4 (financial trade-offs). The language around taxis and food is the most visceral across all interviews — calling parents for taxi money, choosing between lunch and attendance, eating semichka for dinner. These are not incidental details; they indicate that the bus problem intersects with genuine economic precarity.

**Strongest academic consequence cluster:** Cluster 3. GPA drops and missed graded assessments are irrecoverable harms. Tsira's academic disengagement ("What's the point?") suggests the problem is already generating downstream effects beyond lateness.

**Unexpected cluster:** Cluster 7 (students know the solution). We did not expect participants to have already accurately diagnosed the information-vs-infrastructure problem and named what a viable solution looks like. Nino's formulation is a gift — it separates the problem we can solve (information honesty) from the problem we cannot (bus operator performance). One participant also stated explicit willingness to pay.

**Cluster that contradicts original hypothesis:** We may have been framing this primarily as a "bus reliability" problem. Clusters 1 and 7 together suggest the problem is better framed as a "decision-support" problem: students need accurate data at the moment they decide whether to leave, not a faster or more punctual bus. If the information were honest, students say they could cope with delays. It is the false promise of accuracy that prevents rational planning.

## Preliminary Problem Statement (draft)

> KIU students who commute by Bus #3 cannot make reliable decisions about when to leave home because the real-time tracking system consistently provides inaccurate and misleading information, forcing them to build in extreme time buffers that cost them sleep, money, productivity, and in some cases academic standing.

## What We Need More Data On

1. **Willingness to pay:** Nino stated explicitly she would "pay good money" for an honest tracking tool. This is the only payment-intent signal in the dataset. What would she pay, for what feature, in what format? This needs direct follow-up.

2. **Frequency quantification:** Many participants describe the delay and app-failure problem as chronic but we have few specific frequency figures. David's five-hours-per-week calculation is the most precise. We need: how many times per week does the app mislead vs. simply delay? How often does a student pay for a taxi?

3. **Intercity commuter group size:** Nikoloz and Tsira are the only two intercity commuters in the sample. How many KIU students are in this situation? Their failure mode is categorically more severe than city-based students and may warrant a separate solution consideration.

4. **Informal information-sharing systems:** Multiple participants mentioned Messenger groups and Facebook posts about bus delays. Is there already a functional informal tracking system? What are its limitations? Could it be formalised or improved?

5. **Academic policy on transit lateness:** Do any lecturers or faculties formally acknowledge bus delays as a valid excuse? Are there students who have successfully raised this with the administration?
