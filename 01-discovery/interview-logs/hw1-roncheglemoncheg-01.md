# 2. ICP Fit Assessment

- **Fit:** Strong / Bullseye Segment

**Why:**  
Nino is a second-year KIU student who lives off-campus with her family and depends on Bus #3 for daily commuting.

She fits the target user very well because:

- She uses the bus every weekday
- She directly experiences its unreliability
- She has developed coping strategies like waking up early and managing expectations at home

This makes her a high-frequency user who deals with the problem constantly.

---

# 3. Verbatim Quotes

- "My parents keep asking why I wake up so early. I tell them it is for studying but honestly it is because of the bus. I cannot explain to them every day that I might be late otherwise. They would worry."

- "I used to screenshot the app every morning and send it to my mum so she knew when I left. Then one day the screenshot said three minutes and I still waited twenty. She asked me later if I had taken a different bus. I said yes."

- "By the third day I just sat at the stop and kind of laughed... There is a point where it stops being your fault and just becomes the bus's fault and you have to accept that."

- "Honestly, just tell me the truth. Not 'three minutes' when you mean twenty. Not 'on time' when you mean cancelled. I do not need it to be perfect. I just need it to be honest, and I will plan around that."

- "I would pay good money for a bus app that just told the truth."

---

# 4. Problem Exploration Narrative

This interview showed that the bus problem goes beyond transportation—it’s about losing control over your time and daily life.

Nino puts in a lot of effort just to deal with the system’s unreliability. This includes:

- Waking up much earlier than needed
- Managing how her parents perceive her schedule
- Constantly adjusting her routine

One key insight was how bad data creates bigger problems than delays themselves. When the app gave incorrect information, it didn’t just waste her time—it caused misunderstandings with her mother. She even had to lie to explain the situation.

Over time, she has started to accept the situation, even joking about it. But at the same time, she clearly wants a solution. Her willingness to pay shows that this problem has real value to solve.

---

# 5. Mom Test Compliance Check

**Good Example:**  
When Nino talked about sending screenshots to her mother, I didn’t ask something like:  
_"Would a better app help with that?"_

Instead, I let her explain what actually happened (including lying about taking a different bus). This kept the focus on real behavior, not assumptions.

**Needs Improvement:**  
When she said she would "pay good money," I didn’t follow up enough.

According to The Mom Test, statements like this can be unreliable. A better question would be:  
_"What was the last thing you spent money on to solve a travel problem?"_

This would show whether her actions match her words.

---

# 6. Personal Reflection

What stood out the most was how much the social side of commuting matters.

At first, I was focused on technical issues like schedules and tracking accuracy. But this interview showed that the impact goes beyond that. For students living with their families, being late becomes a source of stress at home.

This changed how I see the problem. It’s not just about building a better bus tracker—it’s about helping students stay reliable in the eyes of their parents and professors.

In a way, the solution isn’t just about saving time, but also about protecting trust and reputation.
