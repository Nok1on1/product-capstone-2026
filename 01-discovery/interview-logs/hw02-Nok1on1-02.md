Interview Log 2: Tsira B.

    Interviewer: G Mikaberidze 

    Date: April 8, 2026 

    Duration: 10–15 minutes 

    Interviewee: Tsira B. (Student at KIU) 

    Recruitment: Bandersnatch team fieldwork 

2. ICP Fit Assessment

Fit: Strong. Tsira represents the "extreme" end of the ICP. She commutes from Samtredia, adding intercity complexity. Her commute involves high-stakes transfers where a delay in one city triggers a "cascading" failure in the next, leading to significant financial and academic stress.
3. Verbatim Quotes

    "I just stand there leaning against a wall, hoping the bus shows up so I don't miss my first lecture. It's a long way, man." 

    "I'm standing there, it's freezing or raining... I could be in my bed in Samtredia, but instead, I'm standing on a random sidewalk in Kutaisi..." 

    "I ran to the #3 stop, and I saw the bus pulling away. I waved like a crazy, but the driver didn't give a f***." 

    "I missed half of my seminar. I just walked in, sat in the back, and didn't even open my laptop. What's the point?" 

    "If I spend 6 or 7 lari on a taxi, that's my lunch money gone. I'll be eating 'semichka' for dinner." 

4. Problem Exploration Narrative

For Tsira, the commute is a source of physical exhaustion and "existential" frustration. The lack of coordination between intercity marshrutkas and local buses creates a fragile schedule. When she misses a connection, the consequence is not just lateness but a total loss of motivation—she describes a state of academic burnout where she no longer feels it is worth participating in class after a failed commute. Furthermore, the financial "safety net" (taxis) creates a zero-sum game with her basic needs, forcing her to choose between transportation and food.
5. Mom Test Compliance Check

    Success: I focused on the "cost" of her alternatives. By asking what she does when the bus is "dead," I learned about the real-world trade-off between taxi fares and her lunch money.

    Improvement: I allowed her to express a lot of "complaints" about the weather and the drivers. While good for empathy, I should have steered the conversation back to specific numbers, such as exactly how many times per week she is forced to pay for a taxi.

6. Personal Reflection

The most striking thing I learned was the link between transit and academic disengagement. Seeing a student so defeated that they don't even open their laptop in a seminar made me realize this isn't just a logistics problem; it's a "student success" problem. It changed my focus from simply "tracking buses" to "saving the student's day."
