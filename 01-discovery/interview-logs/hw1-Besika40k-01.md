# 2. ICP Fit Assessment

- **Fit:** Strong / Primary Target

**Why:**  
The ideal participant for this research is a KIU student who depends on public transport daily. Mariam fits this perfectly. She is 19, has been living off-campus for three years, and uses Bus #3 almost every weekday. She has experienced ongoing issues with the system since 2023.

Because she relies on the bus not just for university but also for tutoring work, her experience gives valuable insight into the real impact of the problem.

---

# 3. Verbatim Quotes

- "It is very jam-packed. The bus is late often... There’s not enough air or conditioning going on in there. Especially in the evenings... the buses are really sparse."

- "Sometimes it has happened that there were so many people that I physically could not get on the bus, and then I had to wait for the next bus—me and a lot of other students."

- "I was on Bus number 3 and I had to get down earlier—much, much earlier... five stops early—because I physically could not breathe... I had to get down because I couldn’t breathe and otherwise I was going to faint."

- "I make sure to not schedule any lessons at least two to three hours after I’m supposed to get home, because in that case, I will not be late for my lessons."

- "I feel like I’m constantly in a waiting mode... that time could be spent better if I was using it for other things like work or my classes."

---

# 4. Problem Exploration Narrative

This interview showed that the "Bus #3 problem" is much bigger than just being late. It affects both students’ health and their ability to use their time effectively.

Mariam described extremely crowded buses, sometimes to the point where it becomes hard to breathe. In one case, she had to get off five stops early and walk for about 25 minutes just to avoid fainting.

Another major issue is unpredictability. Because buses are unreliable, she feels stuck in a constant “waiting mode.” To deal with this, she leaves large gaps in her schedule—sometimes up to three hours—just to make sure she won’t be late for tutoring.

This clearly shows that the problem is not only about transportation. It directly impacts students’ productivity and even their income.

Interestingly, she mentioned that university shuttle buses work much better because they follow a strict and predictable schedule. This suggests that the issue is more about poor scheduling and management of the public system rather than the route itself.

---

# 5. Mom Test Compliance Check

**Good Example:**  
When Mariam said the bus is often late, I asked:  
_"Tell me about a recent time when your commute didn't go as planned."_

This worked well because it focused on a real past experience instead of asking for opinions. It led to a detailed and useful story about her needing to get off the bus early.

**Needs Improvement:**  
At one point, I asked:  
_"I guess it's waking up early and going early, right?"_

This was a leading question because I suggested the answer. A better approach would be:  
_"What do you usually do to make sure you arrive on time?"_

This would let her explain her own behavior without influence.

---

# 6. Personal Reflection

The most surprising insight was the idea of “waiting mode.”

At first, I thought the main issue was just the time spent on the bus. But the interview showed that the real cost is much bigger. Mariam has to block out several hours in her schedule just to deal with possible delays.

This changed my perspective. The problem is not just about commuting—it’s about lost time, reduced productivity, and lower quality of life.

It made the issue feel much more serious. Solving this wouldn’t just improve transportation—it could give students back hours of their day.
