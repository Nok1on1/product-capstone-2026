# 2. ICP Fit Assessment

- **Fit:** Strong / Primary Target

**Why:**  
Giorgi is a first-year KIU student who recently moved to Kutaisi and lives off-campus. He relies on Bus #3 as his main way of getting to university.

He fits the target user because:

- He uses the bus daily
- He has already experienced academic impact (missed quizzes)
- He faces financial limits (cannot always afford taxis)
- He is still new, so his reactions are not “normalized” yet

This makes his perspective especially valuable.

---

# 4. Problem Exploration Narrative

This interview highlighted that the Bus #3 issue is not just about delays—it’s about uncertainty, hidden costs, and loss of control, especially for new students.

Giorgi described his commute as a “gamble,” which shows how unpredictable the system feels. Unlike older students, he hasn’t adapted yet, so the frustration is still very clear and direct.

A key insight was the **trust breakdown**. Before moving to Kutaisi, he checked the schedule online and believed it was reliable. But after experiencing reality, that trust quickly disappeared. This shows that the problem starts even before students arrive.

Another important point is the **real cost of commuting**. While the bus is cheaper in theory, Giorgi often ends up paying for taxis or walking long distances. This leads to:

- Financial strain
- Physical discomfort (arriving sweaty to class)
- Academic consequences (missed quizzes)

He is already questioning whether living farther away is actually worth it, which shows how serious the issue is.

Overall, the problem affects not just transport, but also academic performance, finances, and confidence.

---

# 5. Mom Test Compliance Check

**Good Example:**  
I asked about his actual experience as a first-year student commuting in Kutaisi. This led to specific, real examples like missing quizzes and walking long distances.

This follows Mom Test principles because it focuses on past behavior instead of opinions.

**Needs Improvement:**  
I didn’t ask enough follow-up questions about the missed quizzes.

A better approach would be:  
_"Can you tell me about the last time you missed a quiz because of the bus?"_

This would give more concrete data (dates, frequency, impact) instead of general statements.

---

# 6. Personal Reflection

The most surprising insight was how different first-year students experience the problem.

Unlike older students who are used to it, Giorgi still sees the system as unpredictable and frustrating. The biggest shift for me was realizing that the problem actually starts before students even arrive—through misleading schedules and false expectations.

It also made me think differently about cost. I assumed buses are always the cheaper option, but in reality, when you include taxis and lost opportunities, that might not be true.

This changed my perspective from just a transport issue to a broader problem involving trust, money, and academic performance.
